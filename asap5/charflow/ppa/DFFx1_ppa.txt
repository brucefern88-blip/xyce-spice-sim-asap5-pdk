Cell: DFFx1
Area: 396nm x 140nm = 55440 nm^2 (9 CPP)
Transistors: 16T (8 NMOS + 8 PMOS, all nfin=2)
Topology: Master-slave TG DFF (INV_CLK + TG1+INV1 + TG2+INV2 + INV_OUT)

Extraction: Hand-crafted subcircuit with analytical parasitic estimates
  Cext_D=0.86fF, Cext_CLK=0.92fF, Cext_CLKb=2.12fF
  Cext_ML=1.81fF, Cext_MI=1.81fF, Cext_SL=2.02fF
  Cext_SI=1.29fF, Cext_Q=1.21fF

Simulator: Xyce DEVELOPMENT-202603192040, BSIM-CMG level=107
Models: ASAP5 GAA nanosheet, TT corner, calibrated
Load: 1.0 fF, CLK edges: 5ps (0.7V) / 10ps (0.4V)
Init: .IC on internal nodes + 2 CLK warmup cycles with D=0

  EDP improvement: 0.36x (worse at 0.40V)

=== Design Notes ===
OUTPUT SWING WARNING: The DFF output does not reach rail-to-rail at either
voltage. The estimated parasitic capacitances on internal nodes (CLKb=2.1fF,
SL=2.0fF, ML/MI=1.8fF) are large relative to the nfin=2 drive strength.
The TG feedback loops create contention that, combined with these parasitics,
prevents the latch nodes from resolving to full supply/ground within the
clock half-period.

At 0.7V, the swing is 0.24-0.56V (320mV, 46% of VDD) -- marginal but
the 50% threshold crossings are valid for CLK-to-Q measurement.

At 0.40V, the swing is only 0.18-0.28V (100mV, 25% of VDD) -- the DFF
is barely functional. Near-threshold operation requires either:
  1. Larger transistors (nfin=3 or nfin=4) for stronger drive
  2. Reduced parasitic capacitances (actual layout extraction vs estimates)
  3. Longer clock periods to allow full state resolution

The high parasitic capacitances also explain the large energy/cycle (3.5 fJ
at 0.7V) compared to a simple inverter (0.6 fJ) -- the DFF has 8 internal
nodes with ~12 fF total estimated parasitic load being charged/discharged.
